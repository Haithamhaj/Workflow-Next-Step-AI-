## **2\. Core Problem the Project Solves**

The core problem is not simply that companies lack AI tools.  
The real problem is that many organizations attempt to buy, deploy, or pilot AI on top of unstable operational reality.

What often happens in practice is this:

1. A company wants AI or analytics value.  
2. That value depends on usable data.  
3. Usable data depends on cleaner and more consistent operational execution.  
4. Consistent operational execution usually depends on workflow clarity, role clarity, decision clarity, and better process control.  
5. But many companies do not actually have that clarity in a form that reflects reality.  
6. As a result, AI tools and POCs are often placed on top of weak foundations.

This means the problem is not only “bad data.”  
It is often **bad upstream operational structure**.

If operational work is inconsistent, undocumented, dependent on tacit human judgment, or misaligned with reference documents, then the data generated by that work will also be inconsistent, incomplete, or misleading.

Trying to fix AI at the top layer without fixing operational reality below it leads to shallow or unstable outcomes.

This project therefore treats workflow reconstruction and documentation correction as the missing intermediate layer between:

* operational chaos  
* weak automation readiness  
* poor data quality  
* and disappointing AI adoption

The product exists to help organizations move from real-world operational behavior to structured workflow understanding, then to corrected documentation, and only after that toward stronger automation and more reliable AI usage.

## **3\. Strategic Product Thesis**

Organizations often try to use AI, analytics, or digital tools on top of processes that are poorly documented, partially understood, or inconsistently executed.

When the underlying operational workflow is unclear, fragmented, or dependent on undocumented human judgment, the resulting data is also weak. That weak data then limits dashboards, automation, and AI outcomes.

This product is designed to solve that upstream problem.  
It captures operational reality as it is actually performed, reconstructs it into structured workflow logic, compares it against available references when useful, and generates corrected or missing documentation.

Its role is not to execute automation itself.  
Its role is to create the missing operational clarity required before automation, clean process instrumentation, and more meaningful AI adoption can succeed.

## **5\. Product Definition**

This project is not an automation engine in its first version.  
It is also not a chatbot product in its first version.

The first product is an **analytical engine** whose main purpose is to produce a high-value **document output**.

That engine is intended to do the following:

1. Capture how work is actually being done from employee explanations and other available inputs.  
2. Reconstruct the workflow in a structured and traceable way.  
3. Compare reality against available references such as SOPs, policies, SLAs, questionnaires, role documents, or other sources.  
4. Detect contradictions, structural gaps, process weaknesses, decision ambiguity, and reference weaknesses.  
5. Produce a workflow output that reflects reality more accurately.  
6. When references are weak or missing, produce a documentation path in parallel, such as recommendations or draft documents that better match current operational reality.

This means the first product is a **pre-execution intelligence layer**.  
It prepares the ground for later automation, AI usage, process redesign, SOP rewriting, SLA refinement, or policy revision.

It does **not** perform technical execution design such as API design, RPA design, or implementation architecture in version one.

## **8\. Core System Logic**

### **Workflow Output Layers**

The system should not produce only a single workflow view.  
It should produce multiple layers.

#### **Required Workflow Layers**

1. **Workflow as Described**  
   The workflow as explained by the employee or source actor.  
2. **Reference View**  
   The workflow, rules, or expectations implied by available references such as SOP, SLA, policy, or other operational documents.  
3. **Gap Analysis**  
   The gap between reality and reference, or between parts of the workflow itself.  
4. **Decision Logic and Exception Layer**  
   The conditions, branches, rules, governance, allowed paths, prohibited paths, and exception handling logic.  
5. **Proposed Workflow v1**  
   A first reconstructed workflow before later clarifications are incorporated.  
6. **Open Questions / Clarification Layer**  
   Questions, ambiguities, assumptions, and unresolved items that may influence the current version.  
7. **Updated Workflow Version**  
   A newer version built after additional answers or information are provided.

### **Important Simplification That Was Accepted**

The system should **not** classify open questions into "major" and "minor" categories for now.  
That would add unnecessary complexity.

Instead:

* the current version remains valid as the current best summary  
* if additional answers arrive later, the system builds an updated version

This means the logic is based on **versioning**, not on heavy question categorization.

## **9\. Output Philosophy**

A major design decision was made here.  
The system must work in **both directions**.

### **Direction A — Reference to Reality**

If a useful reference exists:

* analyze the reference  
* compare it with actual practice  
* detect gaps  
* produce workflow and findings

### **Direction B — Reality to Reference**

If the reference is weak, generic, missing, or structurally insufficient:

* do not stop the analysis  
* build the workflow from employee reality  
* use the approved workflow output to recommend or draft the missing documentation

This means weak reference quality does **not** stop the system.  
It only changes the type of output.

### **Practical Meaning**

When reference quality is strong:

* workflow analysis remains the main path

When reference quality is weak:

* workflow analysis still happens  
* a documentation path is added in parallel

This documentation path may include:

* document recommendation  
* document review recommendation  
* document rewrite recommendation  
* document draft generation

## **10\. Recommendation vs Draft Logic**

This distinction is now formally important.

### **Document Recommendation**

This is a **diagnostic output**.  
It is used when the evidence is not yet mature enough to responsibly generate a full draft.

The system may say:

* what document is needed  
* why it is needed  
* what purpose it should serve  
* what it should contain  
* what currently prevents proper drafting

### **Document Draft**

This is a **constructive output**.  
It is used only when the workflow evidence is mature enough to support an actual first draft.

Examples:

* SOP draft  
* SLA draft  
* policy draft  
* questions set draft

### **Rule That Was Accepted**

**Recommendation comes first by default. Draft comes only when evidence is mature enough.**

### **Draft Versioning Behavior**

* **Draft v1** is optional after **Proposed Workflow v1** if the current evidence is already mature enough to justify an early draft.  
* The system should ask the user whether they want to see that draft.  
* This early draft must be clearly labeled as conditional and pre-clarification.

Suggested label:

* **Draft v1 — Pre-clarification / Conditional**

Then later:

* when more answers or information arrive  
* and the workflow is updated  
* the system may produce the updated documentation version automatically as the next draft version

Suggested label:

* **Draft v2 — Post-clarification / Updated**

### **Important Simplification**

There is no need to create a heavy distinction between “major questions” and “minor questions.”  
The system should simply maintain a current version and then produce an updated version whenever new information arrives.

## **11\. Reference Logic**

### **Benchmark Basis Accepted as Initial Foundation**

The following were accepted as the initial foundation for evaluating reference quality:

* **ISO 9001** for documented information and process-oriented discipline  
* **BPMN** for workflow and process representation logic  
* **APQC PCF** for process classification and shared process language  
* **Internal baseline criteria** specific to this project  
* **Domain lens** to be added later depending on the sector and use case

### **Important Principle**

The system should not blindly assume a document is wrong just because it is generic.  
A document may be generic **by design**.  
But it may still be **insufficient for the current analytical purpose**.

That distinction is critical.

### **Document Type Definitions**

The system should not classify documents based on their visible labels alone.  
A company may call something an SOP when it behaves more like a policy, or call something an SLA when it is actually a measurement sheet.

#### **Core Rule**

**Declared label is not the main basis of classification. Content and function are.**

#### **Document Types Defined So Far**

**1\. SOP (Standard Operating Procedure)**  
A document that describes how an operational process or recurring procedure is executed.  
It is appropriate when the company wants consistency in execution and reduced variation.

What it usually should contain:

* purpose  
* scope  
* core steps  
* responsibilities  
* decision points if relevant  
* exceptions if relevant  
* related controls or rules

It should not be treated as a full operational reference if it is too generic, principle-only, or not role-differentiated.

**2\. Policy**  
A document that defines rules, principles, boundaries, or required compliance behavior.  
It is appropriate when the company wants to control behavior, decisions, or compliance rather than explain everyday step-by-step execution.

It should not be treated as a detailed workflow reference unless it actually contains that level of operational logic.

**3\. SLA (Service Level Agreement / Service Standard Reference)**  
A document or reference that defines service levels, expected performance, timing, quality, or service commitments.  
It is appropriate for measuring expected service performance, not for replacing workflow execution logic.

**4\. Questionnaire / Questions Set**  
A structured set of questions used for discovery, audit, elicitation, or information capture.  
It is appropriate when the goal is intake, exploration, validation, or structured information gathering.  
It may be a useful reference source, but not necessarily a workflow reference.

**5\. Role / Responsibility Document**  
A document that defines role scope, responsibilities, boundaries, ownership, and authority.  
It is useful for role clarity but does not replace workflow logic.

**6\. Work Instruction**  
A task-level instructional document that explains how to perform a specific task in a more direct, detailed, and execution-oriented way than a general SOP.

**7\. Process Description / Workflow Description**  
A document that explains how work flows across stages, roles, or systems.  
It may include sequence, handoffs, decision points, exceptions, and system references.

### **Additional Principle**

A document may be **hybrid**.  
Therefore the system may eventually need:

* a **primary functional classification**  
* and a **secondary functional classification**

This is more realistic than forcing every document into a single rigid category.

### **Performance vs Workflow Reference Rule**

The system must distinguish between:

* **Operational Documentation** — how work is done  
* **Performance Documentation** — how outcomes are measured  
* **System / Development Documentation** — how a system or development structure is defined

**KPI / dashboard / Power BI documentation should be treated as performance reference, not workflow reference, unless it also contains workflow logic.**

## **12\. Universal Baseline Criteria**

Once the system functionally classifies a document, it evaluates it using a shared baseline.  
These criteria are not meant to produce simplistic “good/bad” judgments.  
They are meant to determine whether the document is clear enough, complete enough, and suitable enough for the analytical task.

### **The Baseline Criteria**

1. **Purpose Clarity**  
   Does the document clearly state why it exists?  
2. **Scope Clarity**  
   Does the document clearly define what it applies to?  
3. **Intended Audience / Role Coverage**  
   Who is the document for?  
4. **Detail Level Appropriateness**  
   Is the level of detail appropriate for the document type and for the current analytical need?  
5. **Responsibility Clarity**  
   Does the document make responsibility clear?  
6. **Inputs / Outputs Clarity**  
   Does the document show what starts the process or step and what output or state should result?  
7. **Sequence / Flow Visibility**  
   Does the document reveal sequence or process flow?  
8. **Decision / Exception Coverage**  
   Does the document adequately reveal decision conditions, branches, exception handling, or escalation logic when appropriate for its type?  
9. **Governance / Controls Presence**  
   Does the document show controls, constraints, approval rules, policy triggers, or compliance boundaries?  
10. **Comparison Readiness**  
    Is the document actually suitable for comparison against reality?

### **Working Interpretation**

These criteria do not judge the document in isolation only.  
They judge the document’s suitability for the analysis we want to perform.

## **13\. Reference Suitability Scoring**

After functional classification and baseline evaluation, the system should issue a practical usability judgment.

### **Accepted Suitability States**

1. **Highly Suitable for Detailed Comparison**  
2. **Suitable for General Comparison Only**  
3. **Too Generic for Operational Comparison**  
4. **Structurally Insufficient**  
5. **Review Recommended Before Use**

### **Important Clarification**

This scoring is not an absolute judgment of document quality.  
It is a judgment of **fitness for the current analytical purpose**.

## **15\. Operational Pipeline — Reference Analysis**

### **Use Case Boundary Rule**

Use case boundaries should not be treated as universally fixed theoretical definitions.  
They should be determined in the following order:

1. **Company-specific definition first**  
   If the company already defines the operational boundary of the selected use case, that boundary should be captured and used.  
2. **Observed operational reality second**  
   If the company does not provide a formal boundary, the system should infer the working boundary from how the roles actually describe the flow in practice.  
3. **Generic fallback definition third**  
   If neither a formal company definition nor a sufficiently clear operational boundary is available, the system may apply a temporary generic boundary for analytical purposes only.

#### **Current Generic Fallback for Client Onboarding**

For current working purposes, **Client Onboarding** may be treated as:

* from the first meaningful client contact or first active commercial engagement  
* until the first successful operational activation / go-live / initial service start

This fallback must be treated as a temporary analytical boundary, not as a universal truth across all companies.

#### **Important Rule**

The system should explicitly record whether the current use case boundary is:

* company-defined  
* reality-inferred  
* or generic fallback

This matters because boundary uncertainty can affect comparison quality, workflow reconstruction quality, and later documentation accuracy.

### **Reality Intake Model**

The system should not assume a single input modality.  
Reality intake may happen through:

* voice  
* writing  
* or a combination of both

The product is not primarily a self-serve employee tool in its first intended usage model.  
It is an operator-led system.  
The primary user is the analyst / project owner / interviewer who configures the company context, selects the use case, prepares the participant set, and controls the intake process.

#### **Preloaded Intake Context**

Before any participant session begins, the system should already contain as much of the following as possible:

* company definition  
* department definition  
* hierarchy structure  
* role map  
* participant list  
* selected domain  
* selected use case  
* current use case explanation shown to participants  
* available SOP / SLA / policy / questionnaire / other references  
* known policies, constraints, and prior questions if available

This context should shape extraction and follow-up question generation.

#### **Participant Session Logic**

A participant may be given a link or page where they see:

* a short explanation of the selected use case  
* what kind of operational story is being requested  
* what level of detail is useful  
* that they should describe what actually happens, not what is ideally supposed to happen

The participant may then respond by:

* speaking  
* writing  
* or doing both

#### **Participant-Facing Answer Simplicity Rule**

The full internal interpretation model should not be shown directly to participants.  
The participant-facing experience should remain simpler than the internal analytical model.  
When useful, the interface may provide only a small number of simple helper options such as:

* I do not know  
* This is not my responsibility  
* I do not perform this step myself  
* This is outside my stage

These should support the participant without forcing them to navigate the full internal taxonomy.

#### **Question-Type Policy**

The system should not use one fixed question type for all follow-up questions.  
Instead, it should choose among three approved types depending on the nature of the gap:

1. **Open Question**  
   Use when the system needs a free explanation of a step, a decision, an exception, a handoff, or another operational detail that cannot be safely reduced to fixed choices.  
2. **Open Question with Helper Choices**  
   Use when explanation is still important, but the participant may benefit from a few support options to respond more easily or more quickly.  
3. **Choice-Based Question with Optional Explanation**  
   Use when the goal is to clarify ownership, boundary, visibility, responsibility, or another structural distinction that can first be narrowed through a small set of simple choices.

#### **Question-Type Selection Rule**

The system should choose the question type based on the gap type, not based on one universal interaction style.  
At minimum:

* use **Open Question** for decision logic, exception detail, and complex step meaning  
* use **Open Question with Helper Choices** for mixed explanatory gaps where both narrative and support are useful  
* use **Choice-Based Question with Optional Explanation** for ownership, role boundary, execution boundary, visibility, and stage-boundary clarification

#### **Session Processing Flow**

For each participant session, the system should:

1. capture the raw narrative  
2. convert voice to transcript when needed  
3. preserve the original narrative as source evidence  
4. extract workflow steps, decision points, exceptions, handoffs, systems, controls, and unknowns  
5. detect structural and informational gaps  
6. generate targeted follow-up questions based on:  
   * the participant’s own narrative  
   * the preloaded company and department context  
   * available references  
   * prior known questions or policy logic when relevant  
7. collect follow-up answers  
8. update the participant-level workflow understanding

#### **Important Rule**

Follow-up questions should not be generic conversation fillers.  
They should be targeted questions created from actual detected gaps in the participant narrative and from the preloaded context.

#### **Non-Knowledge, Non-Ownership, and Boundary Response Rule**

Each follow-up question should allow and encourage the participant to answer explicitly if any of the following is true:

* they do not know  
* they do not own that part of the workflow  
* they are not the one who performs that step  
* they do not have enough visibility to answer confidently  
* the step happens after their role ends  
* the step happens before their role begins  
* the step belongs to another team or function  
* they only know the outcome, not the internal process behind it  
* they follow judgment or habit, but do not know the formal rule

The participant should be encouraged to say this directly rather than guess.

Examples of acceptable responses include:

* I do not know  
* This is not handled by me  
* I am not the owner of this step  
* I do not have enough visibility  
* This part happens after my role ends  
* This happens before the case reaches me  
* Another team handles this  
* I know the result but not what they do internally  
* I do it this way, but I do not know if there is a formal rule

#### **Structured Interpretation Categories**

The system should distinguish these answers rather than collapsing them into one generic unknown. At minimum, the following categories should exist:

1. **Knowledge Gap**  
   The participant does not know the answer.  
2. **Ownership Boundary**  
   The participant is not the owner of that part of the workflow.  
3. **Execution Boundary**  
   The participant does not perform that step directly.  
4. **Visibility Limitation**  
   The participant does not have enough visibility into that part of the process.  
5. **Workflow Boundary — Upstream**  
   The step happens before the participant’s stage begins.  
6. **Workflow Boundary — Downstream**  
   The step happens after the participant’s stage ends.  
7. **Cross-Team Boundary**  
   The step belongs to another team or function.  
8. **Outcome-Only Knowledge**  
   The participant knows what result occurs but not how it is performed internally.  
9. **Tacit-Only Practice**  
   The participant performs the work through habit or judgment but does not know the formal rule or policy basis.

#### **Analytical Meaning of These Responses**

These responses should not be treated as failed answers only.  
They are also evidence signals.  
They may indicate:

* a real role boundary  
* a knowledge boundary  
* a handoff point  
* a visibility limitation  
* a governance gap  
* or an unresolved workflow gap that must be escalated, compared across roles, or left explicitly open

#### **Follow-up Handling Rule**

If a participant explicitly indicates non-knowledge or non-ownership, the system should not keep repeating the same question to that participant.  
Instead, it should:

1. record the answer as an explicit boundary or unknown  
2. mark the related item for possible escalation to another role, reference check, or open-question tracking  
3. continue with the remaining highest-value questions for that participant

#### **Output of Each Participant Session**

Each participant session should produce more than a transcript.  
It should produce a structured participant-level record.

#### **Internal Session Output Schema**

The internal participant-session record should be organized into the following seven layers:

1. **Session Context Layer**  
   Includes the identity and configuration context of the session, such as:  
   * session ID  
   * participant ID  
   * participant role  
   * hierarchy level  
   * company  
   * department  
   * selected domain  
   * selected use case  
   * use case boundary source  
   * input modality  
   * session version  
2. **Raw Input Layer**  
   Preserves what was actually provided, such as:  
   * raw audio reference when available  
   * raw text input  
   * raw transcript  
   * cleaned transcript  
   * language  
   * timestamp  
3. **Extracted Workflow Layer**  
   Stores what the system extracted from the narrative, such as:  
   * extracted steps  
   * extracted sequence  
   * extracted handoffs  
   * extracted decision points  
   * extracted exceptions  
   * extracted controls  
   * extracted systems  
   * extracted dependencies  
   * extracted role mentions  
   * extracted unknowns  
4. **Extracted Step Object Rule**

Extracted steps should not be stored as simple loose text lines only.  
Each extracted step should be represented as a structured object whenever enough evidence exists.

At minimum, each step object should aim to capture:

* step ID  
* step name  
* actor  
* trigger / input  
* action  
* output / outcome  
* decision point if present  
* exception if present  
* system or tool if present  
* dependency if present  
* evidence note

Additional fields may be added later if needed, but the system should begin with this structured minimum rather than treating steps as unstructured text.

#### **Field Completeness Status Rule**

The presence of a field inside an extracted step does not automatically mean that the field is clear, complete, or reliable enough for downstream use.  
Each important field should carry a completeness status so the system can distinguish between strong extraction and weak or unresolved extraction.

At minimum, the following statuses should be supported:

* **clear** — the field is stated clearly enough for current analytical use  
* **vague** — the field is explicitly mentioned but still too ambiguous or underspecified  
* **inferred** — the field was derived by the system from context rather than stated directly  
* **unresolved** — the field remains insufficiently known and still requires clarification, escalation, or open-question tracking

#### **Follow-up Trigger Rule**

If a field is marked as **vague** or **unresolved**, the system should not treat it as fully complete.  
Instead, that field should become a candidate for targeted follow-up, unless the participant has already explicitly indicated non-knowledge, non-ownership, or another valid boundary condition.

Examples:

* “If the price is sensitive” should not be treated as a complete decision rule.  
* “Sensitive” should trigger clarification about threshold, condition, ownership, or policy basis.  
* If the participant cannot clarify and explicitly indicates that this is outside their knowledge or ownership, the field should remain structured as unresolved or boundary-linked rather than being forced into a false rule.  
4. **Follow-up Layer**  
   Stores the targeted clarification process, such as:  
   1. follow-up questions  
   2. question type  
   3. target gap type  
   4. helper choices when relevant  
   5. why the question was asked  
   6. follow-up answers  
   7. participant response mode  
   8. answer interpretation category  
5. **Boundary and Unknown Interpretation Layer**  
   Stores the structured interpretation of explicit non-knowledge, non-ownership, and boundary-related answers, such as:  
   1. knowledge gaps  
   2. ownership boundaries  
   3. execution boundaries  
   4. visibility limitations  
   5. upstream boundaries  
   6. downstream boundaries  
   7. cross-team boundaries  
   8. outcome-only knowledge  
   9. tacit-only practice  
6. **Analytical Status Layer**  
   Stores the current analytical state of the session, such as:  
   1. participant workflow version  
   2. unresolved items  
   3. escalation candidates  
   4. reference-check candidates  
   5. comparison readiness  
   6. drafting-readiness signal  
7. **Confidence and Evidence Layer**  
   Stores early non-final quality markers, such as:  
   1. evidence sources in session  
   2. explicit-vs-inferred flags  
   3. confidence notes  
   4. ambiguity notes  
   5. contradiction notes  
   6. owner-review-needed points  
8. **Evidence Note Taxonomy — v1**  
   The system should distinguish the source type of evidence behind an extracted step or an important field inside that step.  
   At minimum, the following evidence types should be supported:  
   1. **explicit\_participant\_statement**  
      The participant stated the information directly in the session narrative.  
   2. **participant\_followup\_confirmation**  
      The information became clearer or was confirmed through a targeted follow-up question rather than through the first-pass narrative alone.  
   3. **contextual\_inference**  
      The system inferred the information from context rather than receiving it as a direct statement.  
   4. **explicit\_non\_knowledge\_or\_boundary\_signal**  
      The participant explicitly stated that they do not know, do not own, do not perform, or cannot see the relevant part of the workflow.  
   5. **cross\_participant\_pattern**  
      The same workflow element, rule, or pattern appears across multiple participant sessions.  
   6. **reference\_supported\_evidence**  
      The extracted information is also supported by an available reference source, such as an SOP, SLA, policy, or related document.  
9. **Important Rule for Evidence Types**  
   Evidence type and confidence should remain separate concepts.  
   Evidence type answers the question: **where did this information come from?**  
   Confidence answers the question: **how strong does this information currently appear to be?**  
   A step or field may carry more than one evidence type at the same time.

#### **Important Rule**

These seven layers should remain distinct.  
The system should not collapse raw narrative, extraction, interpretation, and analytical status into one undifferentiated record.

### **Organizational Context Documents**

Not all loaded documents should be treated as workflow references or performance references.  
Some documents function primarily as organizational context sources and should be handled accordingly.

#### **Examples of Organizational Context Documents**

These may include, for example:

* company profile  
* vision / mission material  
* service catalog  
* business unit overview  
* domain or industry description  
* organizational introductions  
* capability summaries  
* client-facing company presentations

#### **Why They Matter**

These documents may not define operational workflow directly, but they can still be important because they may help the system understand:

* the company’s domain  
* the services actually offered  
* the business language used internally and externally  
* the meaning of important terms  
* the boundaries of the selected use case  
* how a workflow fits into the broader operating model

#### **Important Rule**

Organizational context documents should not normally be treated as primary workflow references unless they actually contain operational workflow logic.  
However, they may be used as context-shaping sources that improve interpretation, terminology alignment, and use-case framing.

#### **Recommended Use Mode**

At minimum, the system should be able to classify such documents as:

* organizational context source  
* domain/context support only  
* not suitable as primary workflow reference

#### **Practical Meaning**

If a company profile or similar document is loaded, the system may use it to:

* understand the company’s service model  
* interpret department language and role terms  
* frame the selected use case more accurately  
* improve document classification and workflow interpretation

But it should not mistake this kind of document for an SOP, policy, SLA, or other operational reference unless the content actually supports that classification.

### **Department Tree–Anchored Role Model**

The system should not rely on generic job-title interpretation alone when preparing participant comparison or workflow analysis.  
Instead, it should use the department tree and role structure already loaded at the beginning of the case.

#### **Required Department Structure Input**

Before participant intake begins, the system should capture the actual department tree as provided by the company or the analyst, including where possible:

* department or unit structure  
* reporting lines  
* manager / supervisor relationships  
* role groupings  
* participant list  
* participant IDs or identifiers  
* which participants belong to the selected use case  
* which participants should receive intake links or requests

This structure should be treated as a foundational input to the case, not as optional enrichment.

#### **Important Rule**

Role understanding, synthesis sequencing, escalation routing, and gap-closure targeting should be grounded in the loaded department tree first.  
The system may still use light functional interpretation when needed, but it should not replace the actual provided organizational structure with generic title-based assumptions.

#### **Practical Meaning**

If the department tree already shows, for example:

* who reports to whom  
* who is a manager  
* who is a supervisor  
* who belongs to communicator, salesman, account management, or another group  
* and who should be included in the selected use case

then this should drive:

* participant targeting  
* grouping for synthesis  
* escalation direction  
* ownership interpretation  
* role-boundary interpretation

The system should build participant grouping and synthesis logic from the actual loaded structure of the department rather than from abstract role labels alone.

### **Hierarchical Intra-Department Workflow Synthesis Model**

For the current project direction, participant comparison should not be treated primarily as an evaluation or ranking exercise.  
Its main purpose is to synthesize the most complete and operationally useful workflow from multiple participants inside the same department and use case.

#### **Core Principle**

The system should compare and synthesize participant inputs in order to reconstruct the strongest workflow reality, not to judge which participant is better or worse.  
Different participants may each hold valid parts of the workflow, and the goal is to combine them into a fuller workflow representation.

#### **Primary Scope**

For the current sales onboarding direction, synthesis should happen inside the sales department first.  
This means the system should begin with the internal participants of the selected department and use case rather than expanding immediately into all connected functions.

#### **Hierarchical Synthesis Order**

The default synthesis order should be:

1. **Peer-level synthesis**  
   Gather and synthesize participants from the same working level or group to build the strongest operational base from their combined workflow knowledge.  
2. **Supervisor-level enrichment**  
   Add the supervisor layer to enrich the workflow with oversight logic, common patterns, approvals, controls, and escalation rules.  
3. **Manager-level enrichment**  
   Add the managerial layer to enrich the workflow with thresholds, policy interpretation, authority boundaries, and broader department-level logic.  
4. **Department-level synthesis**  
   Use the above layers to produce the strongest department/use-case level workflow synthesis.

#### **External Function Rule**

Other functions such as legal, operations, or related teams should not automatically become primary comparison groups in this first synthesis model.  
They should initially be treated as:

* handoff points  
* dependencies  
* external interfaces

They should only become targeted clarification sources when a critical gap cannot be closed responsibly from within the department’s own participants and loaded references.

#### **Important Meaning**

This means the workflow synthesis model is:

* department-contained first  
* bottom-up across hierarchy  
* external-interface aware  
* expansion to outside functions only when critically needed

### **Main Workflow Output Model**

The project should distinguish clearly between internal analytical layers and the main workflow outputs.

#### **Internal Analytical Layers**

These include:

* participant sessions  
* raw narratives  
* transcript and writing inputs  
* participant-level extraction  
* follow-up questions and answers  
* boundary and unknown interpretation  
* cross-role analysis  
* reference analysis

These internal layers support workflow construction, but they are not themselves the main workflow outputs.

#### **Main Workflow Outputs**

The main workflow outputs should be treated as two primary packages:

1. **Initial Workflow Package**  
2. **Final Workflow and Reference Package**

#### **Initial Workflow Package**

The Initial Workflow Package should be produced after the initial analysis of all currently available inputs.  
It is the first full analytical package for the selected department and use case.

#### **Core Composition of the Initial Workflow Package**

The Initial Workflow Package should include the following core sections:

1. **Initial Synthesized Workflow**  
2. **Workflow Rationale**  
3. **Workflow Value / Usefulness Explanation**  
4. **Initial Gap Analysis**  
5. **Initial Recommendations**  
6. **Document / Reference Implication** *(conditional / request-based in the first package only)*

#### **Section Contract Rules**

**Sections 1 to 5 are always mandatory.**

**Section 6 is conditional / request-based.** It should appear only when:

* the user/client/operator requested this path  
* or the system explicitly activates it because the case already supports an early documentation implication path

The package should still track the state of this path through a package-level status field such as:

* `not_requested`  
* `not_applicable_yet`  
* `review_recommended`  
* `rebuild_recommended`  
* `conditional_early_draft_possible`

#### **Representation Layers of the Initial Workflow Package**

The Initial Workflow Package should be treated as **one underlying package with two synchronized representations**, not as separate outputs:

1. **Analytical Document Layer**  
   The detailed analytical representation of the package. It explains the workflow, rationale, value, gaps, recommendations, and any active document/reference implication path in fuller form.  
2. **UI Overview Layer**  
   A fast-reading summary representation of the same package. It exists to help the operator quickly understand current status, workflow shape, major gaps, critical unresolved items, and key recommendations before going into full detail.

#### **Important Representation Rule**

The UI Overview Layer is not a separate interpretive output and must not introduce different logic.  
It is a summarized, synchronized representation of the same underlying Initial Workflow Package.

#### **Minimum UI Overview Elements**

The UI Overview Layer should show at minimum:

* package status / progress  
* workflow quick summary  
* key gaps  
* critical unresolved items  
* key recommendations  
* document / reference implication status when relevant

#### **Minimum Section-Level Structure**

**1\. Initial Synthesized Workflow**

Mandatory elements:

* workflow scope / boundary  
* boundary source  
* workflow steps  
* sequence / order  
* decision points  
* handoffs  
* dependencies  
* controls / approvals

Conditional elements when relevant:

* exceptions / alternate paths  
* systems / tools touched  
* known unresolved workflow points

**2\. Workflow Rationale**

This section explains:

* why the workflow was formed this way  
* what signals or evidence most shaped it  
* what major assumptions or interpretation limits still affect it when relevant

**3\. Workflow Value / Usefulness Explanation**

This section explains:

* why this workflow matters  
* how it improves operational clarity  
* how it supports later automation-readiness, cleaner downstream data, and stronger AI readiness

**4\. Initial Gap Analysis**

Mandatory categories:

* critical gaps  
* non-critical gaps  
* unresolved items  
* contradictions

For each item, include at minimum:

* why it was flagged  
* source / signal behind the flag  
* current handling status

Allowed handling-status examples:

* clarified  
* escalated  
* pending review  
* unresolved after escalation

**5\. Initial Recommendations**

For each recommendation, include at minimum:

* recommendation  
* reason  
* expected impact  
* recommendation type

Allowed recommendation-type examples:

* clarification  
* workflow review  
* document review  
* document rebuild  
* follow-up escalation

**6\. Document / Reference Implication** *(conditional / request-based)*

When active in the first package, this section may indicate for example:

* no document action yet  
* document review recommended  
* document rebuild recommended  
* conditional early draft possible if explicitly requested and sufficiently supported

#### **Important Rule for Initial Workflow Inputs**

The Initial Workflow Package should not be built from employee stories alone.  
It should be built from all currently available first-round analytical inputs, including as applicable:

* company context  
* department structure  
* hierarchy information  
* selected use case  
* participant narratives from employees, supervisors, managers, or other relevant roles  
* voice inputs  
* written inputs  
* mixed inputs  
* available policy documents  
* available SOP documents  
* available SLA or service-reference documents  
* questionnaires or intake forms if relevant  
* role or responsibility documents if available  
* any other relevant reference source already loaded into the case

This means the first workflow package should already reflect both:

* reality-side inputs  
* and reference-side inputs

#### **Gap-Closure Transition Rule**

After the initial full analysis is completed, the system should identify the remaining gaps, contradictions, unresolved rules, missing thresholds, boundary ambiguities, or other blockers.

Only the gaps that materially block a sufficiently complete workflow or materially affect final document output quality should be treated as targeted gap-closure items.  
These may be routed to:

* a specific employee  
* a supervisor  
* a manager  
* another function or team  
* or the department as a whole

#### **Critical Gap Definition**

A gap should be treated as **critical** when it prevents the workflow from being sufficiently complete, sequenced, and clear enough to serve as a dependable operational workflow that could later support automation, cleaner downstream data, and stronger AI usage.

At minimum, a gap should be treated as critical if it materially breaks one or more of the following:

* the continuity of the core workflow sequence  
* the clarity of how point A leads to point B and how point B leads to point C  
* the completeness of the conditions required to understand or execute a core step  
* the decision rule or threshold needed for a core branch  
* the handoff or responsibility needed for a core transition  
* the control or approval logic required for a core step  
* the boundary required to understand where the use case actually begins or ends

A workflow should not be treated as sufficiently complete if it cannot be understood clearly enough to be operationally useful and later automation-supportive.

This does not require that the workflow already be fully automation-ready. A workflow may be valid for documentation output — truthfully reconstructed and operationally followable — even when it contains structural ambiguities that would need resolution before automation could reliably execute it. The documentation output is valid at the workflow-clarity level even when automation-readiness is not yet achieved. Non-automatable does not mean workflow-incomplete.

This means the standard is not merely “interesting documentation quality.”  
The standard is whether the workflow is structurally strong enough to be used as a real operational representation that could later enable automation-readiness, cleaner process execution, better data generation, and more grounded AI usage.

#### **Final Workflow and Reference Package**

The Final Workflow and Reference Package should be produced after either:

* no critical gaps are found in the initial package and the workflow is approved for finalization  
* or the targeted gap-closure round is completed for the critical unresolved items

It should include at minimum:

* **Final Workflow Reality**  
  * the strongest workflow version after closure of critical gaps or confirmation that no critical blockers remain  
* **Final Source / Reference Output**  
  * updated source aligned with reality  
  * rebuilt source draft  
  * reviewed source replacement candidate  
  * or another final operational reference output appropriate to the case  
  * this may include SOP, SLA-supporting reference, policy, work instruction, or another relevant operational reference form  
* **Final Gap Analysis**  
  * what was closed  
  * what remains open but non-blocking  
  * what still requires later review if any  
* **Improvement Targets / Final Recommendations**  
  * optional process improvements  
  * target-state suggestions  
  * review points for later enhancement  
* **UI / Overview Layer**  
  * high-level workflow overview  
  * key challenges  
  * major decision points  
  * major handoffs  
  * summary recommendations  
  * short explanation of why the workflow was formed this way and what value it provides

#### **Document Output Timing Rule**

Document-related output may begin after the Initial Workflow Package when the analysis already supports early recommendation, review, or conditional direction.  
However, stronger final document outputs such as a final recommendation, a rebuilt reference, or a final draft SOP / SLA / policy should normally be based on the Final Workflow and Reference Package whenever critical gaps had to be closed first.

## **16\. Workflow Evaluation Logic — Current Direction**

A full final scoring model has not yet been completed, but important parts of it have been defined.  
The current direction is to use a **practical experimental rubric**, not an overconfident final measurement model.

### **Why This Matters**

The project should not pretend to have mathematically perfect scoring before enough real cases have been tested.  
Instead, the rubric should begin as a structured, adjustable working model.

### **Axes Already Accepted or Substantially Defined**

#### **1\. Step Clarity**

A step is clear when it shows:

* who performs it  
* what the action is  
* what input or state triggers it  
* what outcome or output is expected  
* any critical condition needed to understand it

Signs of weak clarity include:

* vague verbs such as “review,” “follow up,” “check,” or “handle” with no operational precision  
* missing actor  
* missing input  
* missing output  
* more than one major action fused into one step  
* hidden tacit knowledge

#### **2\. Sequence Coherence**

A workflow is sequence-coherent when each step logically connects to the next, and the output of one step feeds the following one without hidden gaps.

Key logic accepted:

* if a step ends at point B, the next step should begin from B or from a clearly explained output from that point  
* if a system, API, or external process is involved, that dependency must appear explicitly, not as an unexplained hole

#### **3\. Decision Logic and Exception Clarity**

This has been accepted as a formal rubric axis.  
The workflow must show:

* where decisions occur  
* what conditions trigger each branch  
* what happens if yes / no / alternate condition  
* how exceptions are handled  
* where escalation or fallback paths appear

A key project insight here is that many decision patterns and exceptions live in employees’ heads rather than in documents.  
That is one of the project’s main value areas.

#### **4\. Gap Detection and Source Diagnosis**

The intended logic is:

* detect contradictions  
* detect broken sequence  
* inspect process logic  
* compare reality with reference  
* assess the size and effect of the gap  
* diagnose the likely source of the gap

Gap source types likely include:

* internal workflow gap  
* logic gap  
* reality vs reference gap  
* reference quality gap

#### **5\. Reviewability with Process Owner**

This axis is partially accepted.  
The output should be easy for the real owner or operator to review, correct, and react to.

#### **6\. Pre-Automation Readiness**

This axis is partially accepted.  
The workflow output should not be technical execution design, but it should make later automation analysis easier and more realistic.

### **Important Note**

The rubric is still incomplete.  
But the project now has enough structure to continue refining it systematically.

## **17\. Multi-Employee Analysis Logic**

The project should not reduce multi-employee analysis to a naive rule such as:  
“same gap repeated three times \= document is wrong.”

That is too shallow.

Instead, multi-employee analysis needs structured layers.

### **What Must Be Considered**

* company context  
* department structure  
* role hierarchy and role variety  
* which workflow is shared and which segments are role-specific  
* whether different employees should match fully or only partially  
* whether the document itself was designed for department-wide use or for only certain role levels

### **Important Implication**

If multiple employees show the same gap in the same operational point, this is a strong indicator that the reference may need review.  
However, the system should avoid declaring the reference “wrong” too quickly.  
It should instead say that the reference is **a strong candidate for review**.

### **Additional Project Value That Emerged**

The system may eventually produce not only workflow output, but also:

* SOP review recommendation  
* SLA review recommendation  
* questionnaire redesign recommendation  
* or draft replacements better aligned to current operational reality

This is especially relevant when repeated patterns across employees suggest that the current reference does not match real work.

## **18\. Real-World Example That Influenced the Project**

A real-world sales example from a last-mile environment was reviewed.

### **Summary of the Example**

A sales manager explained the following:

* There is a document for **New Business**, and it is documented around the structure they built.  
* **Account Management** does not have the same kind of document.  
* However, KPIs exist and are documented through Power BI.  
* So the work is documented in some way, but not in the structured workflow-document form originally imagined.

### **What This Changed in the Project**

This example confirmed and strengthened several assumptions:

1. Documentation in departments is often partial, fragmented, or uneven.  
2. The visible name of the reference is not what matters.  
3. Some references describe performance, not workflow.  
4. KPI documentation is not the same as operational workflow documentation.  
5. The system must distinguish between different reference families.

### **New Rule Added Because of This Example**

The system must distinguish between:

* **Operational Documentation** — how work is done  
* **Performance Documentation** — how outcomes are measured  
* **System / Development Documentation** — how a system or development structure is defined

### **Rule That Was Accepted**

**KPI / dashboard / Power BI documentation should be treated as performance reference, not workflow reference, unless it also contains workflow logic.**

2. The first product’s main output is a document.

## **20\. Modular Change Policy**

This project reference is intentionally written so that future changes can be made **locally** without forcing a full redesign of the whole model.

### **How future modifications should be handled**

Changes should be treated as **module-level revisions**, not full-project rewrites, unless a change affects the project thesis itself.

### **Main modules that can be revised independently**

1. **Strategic framing module**  
   * core problem  
   * product thesis  
   * positioning language  
2. **Pilot and domain module**  
   * pilot order  
   * sector focus  
   * domain overlays  
3. **Workflow logic module**  
   * workflow layers  
   * versioning logic  
   * recommendation vs draft behavior  
4. **Reference logic module**  
   * document type definitions  
   * universal baseline criteria  
   * suitability scoring  
   * process structure lens  
5. **Pipeline module**  
   * intake  
   * reference analysis flow  
   * later reality analysis flow  
   * output direction rules  
6. **Evaluation module**  
   * rubric axes  
   * evidence reliability  
   * evidence maturity  
   * weighting logic  
   * multi-employee comparison logic  
7. **Output module**  
   * workflow document outputs  
   * reference review outputs  
   * draft behavior  
   * visual support/UI ideas  
8. **Decision rationale module**  
   * why decisions were made  
   * what risk each decision was solving  
   * what tradeoff was accepted

### **Change control principle**

* A module may be revised without rewriting the whole reference if the change does not invalidate the strategic thesis.  
* If a change affects the strategic thesis, output philosophy, or first-product definition, then wider re-evaluation may be required.  
* Scoring systems, weighting rules, evidence rules, and pilot details are all considered **adjustable modules**.

### **Practical implication**

Future work should be able to:

* refine one section  
* replace one section  
* expand one section  
* or split one section into submodules  
  without resetting the entire project definition.

### **Decision Update — Execution Logic**

#### **Decision: LLM-first with rule gates**

**What was decided:**  
The system should use the LLM as the primary interpretation engine, while deterministic rules enforce hard operational control.

**LLM responsibilities include:**

* narrative interpretation  
* extraction of workflow structure  
* first-pass document interpretation and functional classification  
* follow-up question drafting  
* synthesis drafting  
* contradiction surfacing  
* recommendation drafting

**Deterministic-rule responsibilities include:**

* status transitions  
* blocking logic  
* criticality thresholds  
* package eligibility  
* draft eligibility  
* hard validation checks  
* required field checks  
* gating into or out of final states

**Human review remains necessary for:**

* high-impact ambiguity  
* unresolved contradiction  
* low-confidence high-impact outputs  
* final approval points where the case requires it  
* unresolved core-step ambiguity  
* unclear reference suitability when it affects output direction  
* boundary ambiguity when it materially affects use-case scope or final output quality  
* unresolved sensitive-term clarification after the defined follow-up and escalation path has been exhausted

**Why this decision was made:**  
This operating split uses the strongest real capability of large language models—interpretation, structuring, and drafting—without allowing the model to become the uncontrolled policy engine of the system.

**What problem this solves:**

* reduces build drift  
* prevents unstable policy judgments from being left entirely to the model  
* preserves flexibility in extraction and synthesis  
* improves auditability and consistency in system decisions

#### **Decision Update: Human Review should use a flexible admin-review model**

**What was decided:**  
Human review in this system should use a **flexible admin-review model**. The admin is the single operating owner of the system and may either take a direct action immediately or enter an optional discussion loop with the system before issuing the final action.

#### **Human Review Trigger Reference Set**

The system should raise review items against the following seven-point reference set:

1. high-impact contradiction  
2. low-confidence high-impact classification  
3. Draft v1 readiness decision  
4. Final Package release  
5. unresolved core-step ambiguity  
6. unclear reference suitability when it affects output direction  
7. boundary ambiguity when it materially affects use-case scope or final output quality

#### **Human Review Paths**

When a review item is raised, the admin may choose either of the following paths:

**Path A — Direct action**

* approve the current system judgment  
* override it directly  
* provide direct correction or replacement input  
* request a specific change without extended discussion

**Path B — Optional discussion loop**

* ask follow-up questions about that case  
* provide corrective context  
* challenge the system’s interpretation  
* supply additional evidence or explanation  
* request revision before approval

The discussion loop is available when needed, but it is never mandatory.

#### **Default Review Model**

* the system proceeds automatically unless it hits a review trigger  
* review is exception-based, not universally mandatory at every step  
* Draft v1 cannot be released without explicit admin approval  
* Final Workflow and Reference Package cannot be released without explicit admin approval

#### **Important Rule**

Human review in this system is not only binary validation. It is also an active correction layer when needed. However, the system must still allow lightweight direct action when the admin already knows the correct direction and does not need discussion.

**Why this matters:**

* it prevents over-automation of ambiguous or high-impact judgments  
* it avoids forcing discussion when immediate correction is faster  
* it fits the actual operating reality of a single expert admin reviewing complex cases  
* it preserves review as a real control gate without turning it into unnecessary friction

#### **Decision Update: Core-step identification logic**

**What was decided:**  
A workflow step should be treated as a core step if **any one** of the following conditions is true:

1. it is part of the main path that the use case cannot complete without  
2. it is a major handoff between stages  
3. it is an approval, authority, or threshold decision step  
4. lack of clarity in that step breaks sequence continuity  
5. lack of clarity in that step prevents later automation-supportiveness

**Priority rule when conflict or tradeoff appears:**

* approval / authority / threshold decision steps have higher priority  
* main-path steps have the next highest priority

**Why this matters:**  
This decision turns core-step identification into an operational rule that can now feed:

* critical-gap classification  
* unresolved-core-step review triggers  
* package readiness logic  
* finalization blocking logic

**What problem this solves:**

* prevents vague treatment of what counts as structurally essential  
* gives the system a practical basis for escalating unresolved ambiguity  
* connects workflow importance directly to later automation-supportiveness

#### **Decision Update: Clarification Trigger Logic should be the broader module**

**What was decided:**  
For build clarity, prompt design clarity, and implementation clarity, the stronger structure is **Clarification Trigger Logic** as the parent module. The system should not treat vague or sensitive operational language as a passive note; it should route it through an active clarification path before later unresolved, review, or critical-gap judgment.

#### **Clarification Sequence**

The clarification path should operate in the following order:

1. **Detect** sensitive or vague operational terms, underspecified conditions, unclear approvals, hidden exceptions, ownership ambiguity, or threshold ambiguity.  
2. **Convert** the detected ambiguity into explicit clarification targets.  
3. **Select** the appropriate approved follow-up question type.  
4. **Clarify** with the current participant first.  
5. **Escalate upward through the loaded department tree** when the current participant cannot clarify sufficiently.  
6. **Judge exhaustion** only after the reasonable clarification path has been used.  
7. **Route the remaining issue** into unresolved, review, or critical-gap logic when still materially unresolved.

#### **Minimum Components of Clarification Trigger Logic**

1. **Sensitive / Vague Operational Term Detection**  
   Detect terms and phrases that signal missing thresholds, missing operational definitions, unclear approvals, or hidden exceptions.  
2. **Clarification Target Formation**  
   Convert the detected ambiguity into explicit clarification targets rather than leaving it as vague narrative text.  
3. **Question-Type Selection Logic**  
   Choose the correct approved follow-up style:  
   * open question  
   * open question with helper choices  
   * choice-based question with optional explanation  
4. **Hierarchy-Based Clarification Escalation Path**  
   If the current participant cannot clarify, the system should escalate clarification upward through the loaded department tree according to the actual role hierarchy already registered in the case.  
5. **Exhaustion Rule Before Unresolved Judgment**  
   Only after the defined clarification path has been reasonably exhausted should the issue become a later unresolved item, review item, or critical-gap candidate when relevant.

#### **Hierarchy-Based Clarification Escalation**

**What was added:**  
The system should not escalate clarification randomly or generically. It should use the department tree loaded at case setup and move upward through the actual hierarchy relevant to the selected use case.

**Practical direction accepted so far:**

* begin with the lower operational layer first  
* if unresolved, escalate to the next higher level  
* if still unresolved, escalate again upward  
* continue until the issue is resolved or the relevant clarification path is exhausted

**In the current sales onboarding direction, this may mean sequences such as:**

* salesperson / communicator / account-facing operator first  
* then senior or supervisor layer  
* then manager layer  
* then, only if required, route to the relevant external interface or keep as unresolved review-linked logic

**Important rule:**  
This escalation path is not the same as general workflow synthesis. It is a targeted clarification path used when lower layers do not sufficiently define a threshold, exception, approval condition, owner, handoff rule, or another operationally significant ambiguity.

#### **Sensitive-Term Clarification Rule**

Before the system classifies a workflow issue as a later-stage unresolved item or critical gap, it must first perform targeted clarification when participant language contains vague or sensitive operational terms.

**Examples of such terms include:**

* sensitive price / low price / special pricing  
* big client / strategic client  
* urgent case  
* special case  
* according to the case  
* if needed  
* if approved  
* normal / not normal  
* exception  
* high volume / important account

**Operational rule:**

1. detect the sensitive or underspecified term  
2. convert it into a clarification target  
3. generate the appropriate follow-up question using the approved question-type policy  
4. if the participant cannot clarify, escalate the clarification upward through the defined hierarchy when appropriate  
5. if the hierarchy still cannot clarify it, keep it explicitly unresolved and route it into review / gap logic

#### **Critical Gap Rule After Clarification Exhaustion**

**What was decided:**  
A workflow issue inside a core step should be classified as a **critical gap** only if **both** of the following are true:

1. **Clarification Trigger Logic has been reasonably exhausted**  
   This means the system has already attempted targeted clarification and, when appropriate, has escalated upward through the loaded hierarchy without achieving sufficient closure.  
2. **The unresolved issue still materially affects at least one of the following:**  
   * workflow usability  
   * sequence continuity  
   * decision clarity  
   * later automation-supportiveness

**Why this is the stronger rule:**  
This rule prevents the system from over-classifying early ambiguity as critical while still preserving a hard threshold for unresolved issues that remain structurally harmful after the clarification path has been used.

**What problem this solves:**

* prevents premature classification of unclear language as final unresolved logic  
* makes follow-up questioning a real operational repair layer  
* preserves the value of the hierarchy in closing threshold and decision ambiguity  
* improves the quality of later core-step and critical-gap judgments  
* reduces premature critical escalation  
* keeps critical-gap judgment tied to real structural harm, not only to the presence of ambiguity

The purpose is to construct the **strongest complete operational workflow picture** from distributed knowledge across levels.

### **Practical meaning**

The system should collect workflow-relevant information from:

* lower operational layers such as salespeople  
* supervisory layers  
* managerial layers  
* available references when present and useful

It should then use these inputs to build a workflow that is structurally complete enough to explain:

* how point A leads to point B  
* how point B leads to point C  
* how point C leads to point D  
* what conditions, handoffs, approvals, controls, and exceptions shape those transitions

### **Important implication**

The main synthesis question is not:

* who is correct?  
* who is better?  
* who follows instructions more closely?

The main synthesis question is:

* what combination of participant knowledge and available references produces the most complete, coherent, and operationally useful workflow representation?

### **Reference role clarification**

Documents such as KPI sheets, SOPs, or SLAs are not assumed to be authoritative workflow truth automatically.  
They are inputs that may or may not help complete the workflow picture.

The workflow should still be buildable even when:

* no clear workflow document exists  
* documentation is partial  
* documents are performance-oriented rather than workflow-oriented  
* operational logic is held tacitly inside the team rather than written clearly

### **Why this matters**

This clarification prevents the synthesis engine from drifting into employee evaluation logic and keeps it aligned with the project thesis:

* reconstruct workflow reality  
* complete missing operational picture  
* determine whether references support that picture or not  
* produce a stabilizable workflow that may later be formalized or documented

### **Build implication**

Merge logic, contradiction logic, repeated-pattern logic, and escalation logic should all be designed around workflow completeness and structural usefulness, not around judging participant quality or compliance as the primary objective.

## **35\. Hierarchical Synthesis Merge Direction — Working closure draft**

This section records the current working direction for how multi-level participant inputs should be merged into a single workflow construction path.

### **A. Base-path formation from the lower operational layer**

The system should begin by identifying the strongest common operational path from the lower execution layer first, such as salespeople or equivalent frontline roles in the selected use case.

This common path should be treated as the first candidate base workflow, because it best reflects day-to-day operational execution.

### **B. Difference handling at the peer layer**

Differences across peer participants should not immediately be treated as employee error or final contradiction.

Instead, the system should:

1. identify the common/shared path  
2. isolate the differences  
3. check whether the differences materially affect workflow closure or completeness  
4. carry forward only the materially relevant differences for later clarification or enrichment

### **C. Materiality rule for peer differences**

A peer-level difference should be treated as materially relevant when it affects one or more of the following:

* sequence continuity  
* a required transition between steps  
* handoff clarity  
* decision logic  
* approval/control logic  
* exception handling  
* ability to produce a sufficiently complete workflow

If a difference does not materially affect workflow closure or structural usefulness, it may remain visible in the analytical layer without blocking workflow construction.

### **D. Upward enrichment logic**

When peer-level information is incomplete, vague, or materially different, the system should seek closure from higher or adjacent relevant sources such as:

* supervisor layer  
* manager layer  
* adjacent role at similar level when relevant  
* legal / external function / department owner when the issue belongs there

The purpose of upward routing is not to replace the lower-layer operational picture automatically. The purpose is to close missing rules, thresholds, ownership, approvals, boundary conditions, or other structurally important gaps.

### **E. Closure expectation**

The system should continue gathering relevant inputs until the workflow becomes sufficiently closed and structurally understandable.

The expected end state is not perfect unanimity. The expected end state is a workflow that is complete and clear enough to be operationally useful and later stabilizable.

### **F. Role of the operator/admin**

When material differences remain, they should appear clearly in the report to the operator/admin, including:

* what the difference is  
* why it matters  
* what it blocks or affects  
* what potential closure paths exist

The operator/admin may then:

* review the case through the report  
* add off-system clarification from meetings or consultations  
* provide direct corrective context  
* request targeted follow-up  
* instruct the system to finalize the workflow in a specified direction when justified

### **G. Current boundary of this closure**

The system now has a clarified operational rule for when a remaining difference is treated as blocking versus non-blocking.

### **H. Decision Update — Blocking threshold for remaining synthesis differences**

**What was decided:**  
A remaining difference after reasonable synthesis and closure attempts should be treated as a **critical blocker** when it prevents the workflow from completing its essential agreed structural points.

In practical terms, it becomes critical when the unresolved difference still breaks one or more of the following:

* sequence continuity  
* a required core transition  
* an essential handoff  
* an essential decision rule  
* an essential approval/control point  
* the ability of the workflow to remain complete across its necessary core points

**If the remaining difference does not break workflow completion:**  
It should not be treated as a finalization blocker.  
Instead, it should remain as a **review item / notes layer**, meaning:

* the workflow may continue  
* the issue remains visible in the report  
* the operator/admin may review or improve it later  
* the issue may be adjusted later without preventing current workflow release when the workflow is otherwise sufficiently complete

### **I. Working interpretation**

The distinction is therefore:

* **Critical blocker** \= breaks completion of the workflow’s essential structure  
* **Review item** \= affects quality, refinement, or detail, but does not prevent the workflow from being sufficiently complete and understandable

### **J. Practical implication**

This rule connects synthesis differences directly to workflow completeness rather than to disagreement by itself.  
The system should not stop merely because people differ. It should stop only when the unresolved difference still prevents a sufficiently complete, coherent, and usable workflow from being formed.

### **K. Remaining refinement note**

This closes the main blocking-threshold philosophy for synthesis. A later refinement may still define more explicit machine-level tagging for:

* blocking synthesis issue  
* non-blocking visible difference  
* review-note difference  
* closure-complete difference

But the main build-relevant judgment rule is now materially clarified.

## **36\. Output-State Clarification — As-Is workflow versus improved target-state output**

This section records an important clarification about the relationship between real current workflow and improved/best-practice workflow.

### **A. Core distinction**

The system should not confuse:

1. **current operational reality**  
2. **improved / target-state / recommended workflow**

The current workflow must reflect what actually happens in practice.  
The improved/target-state workflow may reflect a better, more disciplined, more documentable, or more theoretically correct version that aligns with stronger reference logic or best-practice criteria.

### **B. Why this distinction matters**

Many companies operate with partial, inconsistent, weakly documented, or tacit workflows.  
If the system rewrites reality directly into an idealized process, it will lose diagnostic truth.  
If the system shows reality only and never proposes a stronger version, it will lose improvement value.

Therefore both layers may be needed.

### **C. Practical example direction**

For example, the current reality may be:

* salesperson calls the client  
* salesperson visits the client  
* information is handled informally

A more improved / target-state workflow may recommend that:

* the call is documented in a defined way  
* an email confirmation is sent  
* a meeting/call summary is recorded  
* the next step is scheduled through a calendar or tracked mechanism

This improved version is not the same as the current reality. It is a recommended stronger workflow state.

### **D. Output implication**

The system should preserve the real workflow as the primary truth layer for analysis.  
Then, when justified, it may add an improved / target-state recommendation layer that shows how the workflow could be made more robust, more governable, more documentable, or more aligned with reference/best-practice expectations.

### **E. Important rule**

The improved / target-state workflow must not overwrite or disguise the current reality layer.  
It should remain explicitly marked as:

* improved workflow  
* recommended workflow  
* target-state workflow  
* or another clearly non-current label

### **F. Build implication**

This clarification affects output design because the system may need to present at least two distinct workflow states when improvement value is requested or justified:

* **As-Is Workflow Reality**  
* **Improved / Recommended / Target-State Workflow**

The relationship between these two states should remain explicit so that diagnostic truth and improvement value are both preserved.

### **G. Decision Update — Improved / target-state workflow appears only in the Final Package**

**What was decided:**  
The improved / recommended / target-state workflow should appear **only in the Final Workflow and Reference Package**.

It should not appear as a standard output inside the Initial Workflow Package.

### **H. Why this matters**

This decision preserves a clean separation between:

* the earlier analytical/diagnostic stage  
* the later formalized and improved target-state output

The Final Package is the stage where the workflow is expected to be:

* sufficiently closed  
* formally written  
* improved where justified  
* aligned with the selected use case  
* detailed enough to represent the intended target-state form

### **I. Practical implication**

The Initial Workflow Package remains focused on:

* current synthesized workflow reality  
* rationale  
* gap analysis  
* recommendations  
* conditional document/reference implication when applicable

The improved / target-state workflow becomes part of the later finalized deliverable only, after the workflow is sufficiently stabilized.

### **J. Output meaning**

This means the Final Package may contain a more formal, improved, and target-oriented version of the workflow/use case, while the Initial Package should remain primarily diagnostic and reconstruction-oriented.

### **K. Remaining note**

This materially closes the target-state timing question for the current version of the project.

## **37\. Reference-Set Decision Principle — Functional judgment over file names**

This section records the governing rule for how multiple uploaded documents should be judged as a set.

### **A. Core rule**

Final judgment on the reference set should be **functional, not label-based**.

This means the system should not decide the final documentation shape based on file names such as SOP, KPI, SLA, policy, or steps document alone.

Instead, it should decide based on:

* the actual function of each document  
* the actual contribution of each document to workflow understanding  
* whether the documents are complementary, fragmented, overlapping, or contradictory  
* whether the document set is operationally sufficient as a whole

### **B. Document-level and set-level logic**

The system should therefore operate at two levels:

1. **Document level**  
   Each document is analyzed separately using the agreed logic.  
2. **Reference-set level**  
   The full set is then judged together as a functional documentation structure.

### **C. Practical implication**

A company may upload three files with three different names, but the final recommendation may still be:

* keep them as three documents because their functions are genuinely different  
* consolidate them into a smaller number of cleaner documents  
* rebuild them into a clearer documentation structure

### **D. Important meaning**

This prevents the system from preserving weak documentation architecture merely because the current file labels exist.  
It also prevents the opposite mistake of forcing unnecessary consolidation when the documents actually serve distinct and valid functions.

### **E. Final authority**

The system may recommend the best functional documentation structure, but the final decision may still be adjusted by the operator/admin when justified.

## **38\. Workflow Evaluation Rubric Direction — Practical and non-heavy v1**

This section records the current direction for the workflow evaluation logic.

### **A. Core direction**

The evaluation rubric in version one should be **practical, simple, and operational**, not mathematically heavy or over-engineered.

Its purpose is not to create a complex scoring product by itself.  
Its purpose is to help the system determine:

* whether the workflow is sufficiently complete  
* where the main weaknesses still are  
* what remains missing  
* whether the workflow can move forward operationally  
* what recommendations are needed  
* whether the workflow appears mature enough to support later automation thinking

### **B. Project-context meaning**

The project is primarily an operational/documentation consulting system, not a deeply technical scoring system.

Its larger logic is:

* build clearer workflow documentation  
* make the workflow structurally stronger  
* create conditions for later automation  
* improve the possibility of cleaner downstream data  
* support later AI/analytics readiness through stronger upstream workflow structure

### **C. Evaluation style**

The first version of the rubric should avoid unnecessary numerical complexity.  
It should rely on a small number of practical axes with clear operational judgment states.

### **D. Automation-readiness position**

Automation readiness should **not** be treated as a fully separate primary axis.

Instead, it should be treated as a **derived outcome** from the maturity of the workflow across the main rubric axes.

This means the system should infer later automation-supportiveness from factors such as:

* workflow completeness  
* sequence clarity  
* decision/exception clarity  
* ownership/handoff clarity  
* documentation strength

### **E. Why this matters**

This prevents the rubric from becoming conceptually inflated.  
It keeps automation-readiness tied to workflow maturity rather than pretending that automation is a separate standalone quality disconnected from the underlying process structure.

### **F. Practical rubric axes for version one**

The agreed practical axes for version one are:

1. **Workflow Completeness**  
2. **Sequence Clarity**  
3. **Decision / Exception Clarity**  
4. **Ownership / Handoff Clarity**  
5. **Documentation Strength**

### **G. Judgment states for each axis**

Each axis should use the following operational judgment states:

* **Strong**  
* **Partial**  
* **Weak**  
* **Blocking**

### **H. Axis definitions**

#### **1\. Workflow Completeness**

This axis focuses on whether the workflow contains its essential structural elements from beginning to end, not every small detail.

**Strong**  
The workflow contains its essential elements sufficiently from start to end, and the main path is understandable.

**Partial**  
Most essential elements are present, with limited missing parts that do not prevent understanding the overall path.

**Weak**  
Important parts exist, but core completeness is still not strong enough for the workflow to be treated as a dependable operational representation.

**Blocking**  
A core structural gap still prevents understanding or closing the workflow, such as a missing core step, major handoff, essential approval, or essential start/end boundary.

#### **2\. Sequence Clarity**

This axis focuses on whether the movement from step to step is clear and logically traceable.

**Strong**  
The sequence is clear and logical, and the workflow can be followed easily from one step to the next.

**Partial**  
The sequence is mostly clear, but some transitions still need explanation or tighter wording.

**Weak**  
The linkage between steps is weak, with gaps or unexplained jumps that reduce operational clarity.

**Blocking**  
The sequence is broken to the point that the process cannot be reliably followed across its core stages.

#### **3\. Decision / Exception Clarity**

This axis focuses on whether the main decision points, conditions, and important exception paths are clear enough.

**Strong**  
The main decision points and important exceptions are sufficiently clear, including the main conditions or branching logic when relevant.

**Partial**  
There is a useful general understanding, but some conditions, branches, or exception rules still need refinement.

**Weak**  
Decision or exception logic is present but too unclear, vague, or incomplete for strong operational use.

**Blocking**  
A missing or unclear core decision rule or important exception path still prevents the workflow from being sufficiently complete.

#### **4\. Ownership / Handoff Clarity**

This axis focuses on whether ownership and transition of responsibility between roles are clear enough.

**Strong**  
Core ownership and core handoffs are clear and understandable.

**Partial**  
Most ownership and handoff points are clear, but some still need clarification.

**Weak**  
Only partial understanding exists for ownership or handoff logic, and role boundaries remain unclear in important areas.

**Blocking**  
A missing or unclear core owner or core handoff prevents the workflow from being sufficiently complete or traceable.

#### **5\. Documentation Strength**

This axis focuses on the current usefulness and structural strength of the available documentation in supporting analysis, comparison, and later documentation output.

**Strong**  
Current documentation is functionally useful and supports analysis/comparison clearly.

**Partial**  
Current documentation is helpful in part, but still needs improvement or better structuring.

**Weak**  
Current documentation provides only limited value and needs serious review before it can be relied on comfortably.

**Blocking**  
Current documentation is too weak, too fragmented, too contradictory, or too misaligned with reality to support reference use at core points.

### **I. Status note**

The practical axis layer and judgment-state layer are now materially defined. Derived decision outcomes are recorded in the next section.

## **39\. Decision Outcomes Derived from the Rubric — v1**

This section defines the practical operational decisions that should be derived from the five rubric axes.

### **A. Purpose**

The rubric should not end as descriptive axis labels only.  
It should produce practical next-step decisions that guide workflow progression, review, and finalization.

### **B. Derived outcomes**

The main derived decision outcomes for version one are:

1. **Ready for Initial Package**  
2. **Needs More Clarification**  
3. **Finalizable with Review**  
4. **Ready for Final Package**

Additional derived outputs may also be expressed when useful, such as:

* automation-supportiveness note  
* recommendation intensity  
* review/rebuild emphasis for documentation

### **C. Decision logic — Ready for Initial Package**

The case should be considered **Ready for Initial Package** when:

* no axis is in a blocking state that prevents the workflow from being understood at the current diagnostic level  
* the workflow is sufficiently reconstructable in its current form  
* remaining weaknesses do not prevent an analytical package from being useful

Practical meaning:

* weak and partial areas may still exist  
* review notes may still exist  
* the workflow is not yet necessarily final  
* but the case is mature enough for the initial analytical package

### **D. Decision logic — Needs More Clarification**

The case should be considered **Needs More Clarification** when:

* one or more important axes remain too weak for practical confidence  
* clarification paths have not yet been sufficiently used  
* the workflow can still likely improve materially through more follow-up or escalation

Practical meaning:

* the case should not move forward merely because something was captured  
* the next step is clarification, escalation, or targeted follow-up  
* this state is not yet a final rejection or final block unless the unresolved issue is truly blocking finalization

### **E. Decision logic — Finalizable with Review**

The case should be considered **Finalizable with Review** when:

* the workflow is sufficiently complete and usable overall  
* no remaining issue breaks core workflow completion  
* some visible weaknesses, review items, or quality limits still remain  
* these remaining issues do not justify stopping finalization entirely

Practical meaning:

* the case may move toward finalization  
* operator/admin review remains important  
* remaining issues should stay visible in the final reporting layer  
* final release still depends on review approval where required

### **F. Decision logic — Ready for Final Package**

The case should be considered **Ready for Final Package** when:

* core workflow structure is sufficiently complete  
* sequence is sufficiently stable  
* decision/exception logic is sufficiently closed for the intended use case  
* ownership/handoff logic is sufficiently clear  
* documentation/reference conclusions are sufficiently mature for final output direction  
* no remaining blocking issue prevents final packaging

Practical meaning:

* the workflow is stable enough for the final package  
* the final package may include formalized workflow output and final documentation/reference output when applicable  
* improved/target-state output may now appear according to the already-decided package logic

### **G. Simplified operational interpretation**

The overall intent is:

* **Ready for Initial Package** \= diagnostic packaging is viable  
* **Needs More Clarification** \= workflow maturity is not yet sufficient; follow-up should continue  
* **Finalizable with Review** \= the workflow is mature enough to finalize, but visible review items remain  
* **Ready for Final Package** \= the case is sufficiently closed and stable for final formal output

### **H. Important rule**

The system should not force a false precision ranking across all cases.  
These decision outcomes exist to guide progression and control, not to create cosmetic scoring theater.

### **I. Derived automation-supportiveness note**

Automation-supportiveness should be expressed as a derived note from the five main axes rather than a separate main scoring axis.

Examples of derived interpretation may include:

* **workflow appears supportive of later automation analysis**  
* **workflow is partially supportive of later automation analysis**  
* **workflow is not yet mature enough to support later automation analysis comfortably**

### **J. Derived recommendation intensity**

Recommendation intensity should also be derived from the maturity profile of the case.

Examples may include:

* limited refinement recommended  
* moderate clarification and documentation improvement recommended  
* strong rebuild / restructuring / gap-closure effort recommended
